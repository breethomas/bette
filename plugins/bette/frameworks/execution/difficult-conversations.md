# Difficult Conversations

Practical structures for common PM conversations where the stakes are high.

---

## SBI: Situation, Behavior, Impact

For feedback that lands.

- **Situation:** When and where. Anchors the conversation in a specific moment.
- **Behavior:** What happened. Observable actions, not interpretations.
- **Impact:** The effect. On you, the team, the outcome.

"In Tuesday's sprint review (situation), you presented the timeline without flagging the dependency risk (behavior), which meant leadership left with expectations we can't meet (impact)."

SBI works because it removes judgment. You are describing what happened and what it caused, not what kind of person someone is.

---

## Radical Candor

Two axes: Care Personally and Challenge Directly.

| | Don't Challenge | Challenge Directly |
|---|---|---|
| **Care Personally** | Ruinous Empathy | Radical Candor |
| **Don't Care** | Manipulative Insincerity | Obnoxious Aggression |

Most PMs default to Ruinous Empathy -- caring about the person but avoiding the hard thing. The result is problems that fester.

Radical Candor requires both. "I'm telling you this because I want you to succeed" is not a preamble. It is the actual posture.

---

## Nonviolent Communication (NVC)

When emotions are running the conversation.

1. **Observation:** What happened, stated as fact. No evaluation.
2. **Feeling:** How it affected you. Own it.
3. **Need:** The underlying need that is not being met.
4. **Request:** A specific, actionable ask.

"When the requirements changed twice in one week (observation), I felt frustrated (feeling) because I need stability to plan the team's work (need). Can we agree on a 48-hour hold before finalizing scope changes? (request)"

NVC sounds mechanical in writing. In practice, the structure prevents blame spirals.

## Common PM Scenarios

**Pushing back on leadership direction:**
Frame as questions, not objections. "What would success look like if we went this direction?" Use data. Propose an alternative that meets their underlying goal. Never make it personal.

**Saying no to a stakeholder request:**
Acknowledge the need first -- they are not wrong to want it. Explain the trade-off in terms they care about. Offer an alternative or a timeline. "Not now" lands better than "no."

**Giving hard feedback to a direct report:**
Use SBI. Be specific. Do it privately, soon after the event. State what good looks like. Ask for their perspective -- you may be missing context.

**Navigating peer conflict:**
Use NVC structure even if you do not say it out loud. Separate the problem from the person. Find the shared goal. If you cannot resolve it 1:1, escalate together -- not behind their back.

**Delivering bad news about a project:**
Lead with the impact ("we will miss the date by two weeks"), then the cause, then the path forward. Do not bury the headline. Bring a plan, not just a problem.
